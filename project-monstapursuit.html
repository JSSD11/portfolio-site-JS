MonstaPursuit
Case Study

OVERVIEW

MonstaPursuit is a mobile app that turns your run into a game, where you must find and battle virtual monsters with exercise along your route. The app provides a new way to bring variety and fun into running, as well as providing a social platform to foster local fitness communities.

Introduction

CONTEXT

I designed MonstaPursuit as part of CareerFoundry’s UX Design course, where I was tasked to design a scavenger hunt mobile app. I decided to expand the concept and use it to turn a one-dimensional, individual workout, such as running,into a multi-dimensional, fun, and social experience.

MY ROLE

I was responsible for all stages of the design process, from the initial concept and problem definition, to the implementation of the final user interface.

TOOLS

Sketchpad, pen & pencil, Balsamiq, Adobe XD, Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop, Prott, UsabilityHub, OptimalSort.

THE CHALLENGE

  • Make running a fun and social activity.
  • Provide a way to monitor and view progress.
  • Provide a motivation system that encourages people to reach their goals.
  • Motivate people to be develop healthy habits and exercise regularly.

THE SOLUTION

  • A game where players earn points by battling virtual monsters with exercise along their run.
  • Allow players to challenge each other and share their experience.
  • Players can easily view and analyse their stats.
  • Reward players with points that can be exchanged for real rewards.

PLAY

Have fun being active in the outdoors. Play against yourself or, for added competition, challenge a friend.

ANALYSE

Make the most out of your workouts by setting goals and analysing your stats. Seeing results will motivate you to keep moving.

REWARD

Swap points for special deals from health- and fitness-related goods and services. Treat yourself, you’ve earned it!

Approach

MY PROCESS

I implemented the design thinking process, starting with user research to gain an understanding of user needs and the problem space. This included, conducting competitive analyses, and facilitating user interviews and surveys. Next, I created user personas, task analyses, and user flows, which would become the foundation for the information architecture of the app. The ideation phase entailed the development of low- to mid-fidelity wireframes, which I used to turn into a clickable prototype. These were created using various design software, including Balsamiq, Sketch, and AdobeXD. With the interactive prototype ready, I then conducted usability tests to obtain feedback from potential users. Their feedback was essential in helping me make adjustments to the design and flow of the. I also consulted with colleagues regarding the visual design, whose suggestions were incorporated in the design.

EXPLORING THE PROBLEM SPACE

I first needed to better understand the market in order to make more informed decisions about my strategy and initial minimum viable product (MVP). I started by looking at existing solutions of both fitness and scavenger hunt apps, since my app aimed to combine these two concepts. I was interested in how these apps build engagement and motivation through rewards and gamification, as well as how much emphasis they place on the social features.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I performed in-depth analyses on 5 competitors to identify where they excel, weaknesses in their product, and potential gaps that fail to satisfy customer needs. This analysis was used to form assumptions and a hypothesis for testing.

01/06
Research | User Interviews

GETTING TO KNOW THE USER

The survey provided important initial insights, but I wanted to get to know more about users’ attitudes towards exercise and scavenger hunts, to help define the problem statement. I did this through in-depth interviews where I used the 5 Why’s technique to better understand the needs and frustrations faced by the interviewees.

My research goals were three-fold:

  • Understand why people exercise, what motivates them and what aspects they find challenging in making exercise a regular part of their lifestyle.
  • Understand how respondents use technology while they exercise.
  • Discover the respondents’ experience with scavenger hunts to determine how I could bring together these two concepts.

I recruited 5 people, living in major cities all over the world who enjoyed experimenting with new technology and were generally quite active or looking to build a more active lifestyle.

I exercise to become a better version of myself.

I feel a sense of achievement when I hit my mini-targets and get through a tough session.

I find inspiration from getting recognized by my peers.

KEY FINDINGS

  • Respondents had mixed impressions on the competitive nature of fitness apps; some are motivated by competing with themselves and friends, while others find it demoralizing.
  • Scavenger hunts were recognized as a great group activity for team-building, shared experience etc. but people rarely partook in them - they were viewed as one-off events.
  • Social features play an important role in motivation (e.g. connecting with like-minded people, peer-recognition, finding inspiration & new ideas), but not all respondents were keen to share their own results.
  • What makes exercise a habit: routine; planning ahead; sense of accomplishment; seeing progress; goal setting; variety to prevent boredom and fitness plateau; the feel-good feeling after exercise.

3 | IDEATE

03/06
Ideate | Information Architecture

CREATING THE BLUEPRINT

After having determined the core features of the app, it was time to figure out how to organize the information. To ensure the app is intuitive and easy to use, it was essential that content was structured in a way that best matched users’ mental models. Therefore, I conducted an online open card sorting exercise with 8 testers in order to gather insight into how users conceptualize the content of the app using a tool called Optimal Sort. I would then use the results to revise the preliminary site map so that the content is categorized in such a way that matches users’ mental models.

CART SORT FINDINGS

The results of the card sort revealed the following key points:

  • The 5 distinct categories established by the participants did not diverge too much from my original classifications.
  • A novel concept (such as the running game app) makes grouping topics less instinctive , due to lack of familiarity, which leads to increased diversity in the results.
  • Sample size is relatively small, thus results are not very strong.

The findings from the card sort were used to update the preliminary site map.

03/06

PUTTING PEN TO PAPER

With the foundations hatched out, the next step was to draft out the core structure and navigation of the app. To do this efficiently and under time contraints, I used brainstorming techniques, such as 6-8-5 method (or crazy 8’s) to generate 6-8 ideas in 5 minutes. This allowed me to iterate through many design options quickly.

The paper wireframes progressed from showing only basic content to displaying more detail in order to better understand the high-level functionality of the app.

5| TESTING

05/06
Testing | Usability Tests

PUTTING IT TO THE TEST

It was finally time to test the concept of the app and assess how well users understood its value proposition. I also wanted to examine the ease of use of the app’s core features in order to optimize IA and improve the overall user experience.

I conducted moderated usability tests, 2 in person and 4 online, in which participants were asked to complete a set of scenario tasks. The tests were recorded and points of frustrations, comments, and observations were noted.

RECRUITING PARTICIPANTS

I recruited 6 participants, each having had some experience with running outdoors and using an app to track their runs. They had varied level of experience and fitness as I wanted to test the app on my target group. So my participants included committed runners with a regular training schedule, those who exercise as a social activity, and those new to running.

05/06
Testing | Affinity Map

AFFINITY MAP

The feedback received from the usability tests was used to create an Affinity Map, with the findings categorized into observations, positive quotes, negative quotes, and errors. These were then collated into a “rainbow spreadsheet” to gauge importance and level of severity according to Jacob Nielsen’s error scale.

KEY FINDINGS

Key findings and observations are summarized below.

  • The concept was well received and all participants were able to correctly describe the purpose of the app.
  • Language choices are key. Copy needs to be clear and to the point.
  • Use familiar design patterns wherever possible.
  • Most of the confusion related to the task of creating a new Mission, due to lack of familiarity with this process. However users noted that after doing it once, the next time it would be easier, signifying good learnability.
  • Work needs to be done on navigation and discoverability.
  • Testers all had similar issues and questions.
  • Simplify, simplify, simplify!
ALL participants were excited about the game and thought it was a fun concept.
4 of 6 testers found elements of the copy ambiguous, particularly when creating a new Mission.
33% found the Play screens distracting due to too much clutter (high cognitive load).
5 of 6 participants expressed confusion relating to the user interface having multiple ways of doing the same thing.
83% liked being able to choose their own exercises and customize the experience.
1 tester suggested to provide personalized plans for users new to exercise who would not know where to begin in creating a Mission.

It’s great! It gets you off the couch in the outdoors and you can compete with your friends.

05/06
Testing | RESULTS & CHANGES

CHANGE FOR PROGRESS

Conducting usability tests was the most eye-opening part of the process, as it allowed me to experience the app through the minds of others and provided valuable insights on how it could be improved.

Of the issues that arose, 5 core issues were identified to be improved upon in the next iteration.

ISSUE 1 : Ambiguous language and copy mean that users had difficulty in sending a message.

  • Testers did not relate the Feed icon and label with sending a message.
  • It was noted that the word “Post” signifies sending a message to all followers.

SOLUTION

ISSUE 2 : Missions screen needs simplifying and clarifying and language used needs re-thinking:

  • Testers found a lack of clarity regarding the difference between a Mission and a Challenge.
  • General negative response to the word “Mission”, especially when related to exercise due to its connotation with a laborious process.
  • Having multiple ways to play and add to planner (CTA button on Missions card and tab on nav bar) led to confusion.
  • It was noted that the “Exercises” tab was out of place as there are only 2 categories of missions - those played alone and those played in groups.

SOLUTION

* NOTE: 83% of users instinctively chose to play by clicking on the CTA button. Also, playing from the tab on the nav bar requires users to first load a challenge, making this method less efficient as well as redundant. The Exercises section consists of a glossary and explanation of the pre-loaded exercises, as well as the ability to create custom exercises, therefore it makes sense to be located as a stand-alone feature on the nav bar.

ISSUE 3 : Missions (now Challenges) empty state screen has too much information (high cognitive load).

SOLUTION

ISSUE 4 : More guidance is needed when creating a custom challenge.

SOLUTION

CONCLUSION

NEXT STEPS

MonstaPursuit’s design has been validated through user testing, and the wireframes and style guide are ready to hand-off to developers.

Once the app is functional, I will continue to test its usability and make improvements where necessary. I will also explore opportunities to enhance the user experience and increase users’ engagement with the app, with features such as points, goal setting and rewards.

A carefully planned points system in which users can progress up a scoreboard is expected to increase competition among users, helping to promote usage as friends try to out beat one another.

Additionally, a rewards system tied to users’ goals can be implemented, whereby users receive extra points and/or coupons from industry partners as they progress further towards their goal. Points can also be exchanged for vouchers to be used in local health-related businesses. These rewards can help promote local start-ups in the health and fitness industry, and hence strengthen the community of like-minded individuals.

By keeping users motivated to use the app, these features will help to build habit as users start feeling better and seeing progress as a result of consistent activity.

Finally, augmented reality features could be implemented to provide a more immersive user experience.

HYPOTHESIS VALIDATION

Validating the above hypotheses will require first launching the new features, testing and refining them through usability tests, and finally monitoring to see whether there is an increase in app downloads and usage. It would also be good to have a blog where users can add new feature suggestions as well as provide room for improvement.

USER TESTING EVOLUTION

User testing needs to be conducted out in the field. By testing the AR aspects to see if they delight the user or if they feel like a gimmick will be key to this feature of the app.

LEARNINGS

Working on this project allowed me to fully understand the importance of competitive analysis, comprehensive user research, and usability testing.

  1. Competitive analysis ensures sound understanding of the market and existing solutions which helped me recognize successful practices, and, more importantly, areas with gaps in the market. I was able to focus on these as opportunities to develop a design further and hence provide an advantage for my users.
  2. Extensive user research provided me with valuable knowledge about my users - who they are and what are their needs - which was essential for delivering a high quality design speecificallly targeted for them.
  3. Usability testing prevented me from implementing inefficient solutions in thee final development stage, thereby saving time, effort, and resources put into future reworks.

While the entire project was a learning experience, I particularly enjoyed listening and talking to users to connect with their motivations and needs to design solutions. It helped me to understand my strengths and weaknesses as a designer which I can now respectively hone and improve upon.
