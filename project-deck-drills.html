Deck Drills Case Study

OVERVIEW

Deck Drills is a mobile app that empowers users to learn new vocabulary in a fun and engaging way. It was developed as part of the CareerFoundry Fundamentals course. The following outlines my process and the outcome of the project.

MY ROLE

I was responsible for all stages of the design process, from the initial concept and problem definition, to the implementation of the final user interface.

TOOLS

Sketchpad, pen & pencil, Balsamiq, Sketch, Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop, Google Forms, Prott.

CHALLENGES

  • People need an intuitive and easy-to-use way to create and customize their own flashcards;
  • ​The​ ​experience​ ​of​ ​memorizing​ ​and​ ​understanding​ ​new concepts,​ ​techniques,​ ​and​ ​terms can feel overwhelming, so people need a way to feel motivated and excited to learn.

OPPORTUNITIES

  • Develop a learning app that is intuitive and simple to use.
  • Provide instant feedback and progress checks to help users effectively and efficiently learn new concepts and material.
  • A key component of learning is enthusiasm, therefore it is important that the app is fun and enjoyable to keeps users motivated and excited to learn.

THE FIVE W's

WHO?

The age range of the target audience is anyone between 14 - 45 years old looking to learn new vocabulary, either for school, work, self-developmnt of simply for fun.

WHAT?

A mobile application focused on learning new vocabulary.

WHERE?

This application is meant to be used on-the-go for a quick 5–10-minute study sessions. The users could use it on the train, on a lunch break or even before bedtime.

WHEN?

Location and context will ultimately depend on the target users. That could be on a bus or train, waiting between classes, in the cafe, in bed, or on a bus.

WHY?

It can be challenging to keep track of all the jargon when studying a new discipline. Students need a way to methodically categorize, reference, and explore new vocabulary and concepts so they can move forward confidently in their field.

1 | DEFINE

DISCOVERING THE PROBLEM SPACE: COMPETITIVE ANLAYSIS

With the project brief outlined, I first conducted a competitive analysis in order to better understand the market that I was designing for and determine what learning apps already exist. I focused my analysis on 3 popular apps: Duolingo, Quizlet, and Anki App, and evaluated the strengths and weaknesses of their user experience. This research helped me to gain insight into the core features of learning apps, and hence helped to inform my design decisions.

2 | Empathise

GETTING INTO THE MIND OF THE USER

In order to conceptualize useful solutions, I defined 4 research goals:

  • Understand the motivations for learning new vocabularly;
  • Uncover what environment/ tools are most conducive to learning;
  • Uncover the pain points when learning something new;
  • Understand what incentivizes and encourages learning.

Research method 1: online surveys

The goal of the online survey was to provide quantitative attitudinal data to be used in conjunction with and support of the qualitative user interviews.

  • 10 questions;
  • 35 respondents;
  • Survey posted on Facebook, Slack, and online communities matching the target user.

Survey findings

  • 36% of respondents learn most effectively through audio assist and speaking methods.
    • Action: explore how such methods can best be incorporated in the app.
  • The majority of respondents that use learning apps (56%) only use such apps on average for 10 - 19 minutes per day;
    • Action: dig deeper into why users are not using learning apps for longer periods. It could be because most users use learning apps as a form of informal learning, in which case they simply use the app when they have some free time as opposed to using it in planned sessions, which suggests a more formal approach to learning with the app.
    • Action: find out what helps to retain users and make it a habit for them to use the app every day.
  • The majoirty of respondents that use learning apps do not us them as their primary learning method - rather they are used as supplementary to going to class or taking an online course.

Research method 2: in-person interviews

In-person interviews were used to gain insights into the needs and wants of learning apps users and to delve deeper into their learning habits, frustrations, and joys when trying to learn new vocabulary. The findings of the interviews informed the ideation phase.

  • 5 interviews were conducted;
  • Respondents were from a range of backgrounds and demographics in terms of age (range btween 22 - 45), gender (3 females and 2 males), occupation (from students to Managrers, to engineers), and location (Egypt, The Bahamas, Spain, UK, and USA).

Summary of interview transcripts and observations:

Key insights

  • It is important for learning apps to incorporate all modes of learning including kinetic, repetition, visual, word association, and experiential learning to suit all types of learners.
  • People are motivated to learn when they can see their progress and, in particular, when they are able to use what they’ve learnt in a real-world setting, e.g. when learning a new language they get excited when they can start conversing with a native speaker.
  • Learning apps offer users the convenience and flexibility to study when they want and in an environment of their choosing.
  • Interviewees mentioned that learning through an app is limited, particularly if it is the only mode used to discover a new field. It is more suited to simply memorizing material, rather than for understanding new concepts due to the lack of interactivity with an instructor or other students.
  • Making learning fun through games and competition can be a successful technique to encourage students to use a leearning app, however it should not be the main focus of the app since it can distract users from the material.
  • Many interviewees highlighted the importance of an immediate feedback loop when it comes to learning a new subject, not simply in terms of informing whether an answer if correct or not, but, more importantly, in providing an explanation for why. Indeed, the lack of instant feedback or inability to ask questions was cited as a major cause for frustration with virtual learning.

The greatest concern regarding learning apps is that they will not provide relevant study material (generally material is found to lack enough depth), leading to time wasting. This highlights the importance of customization based on needs/ interests.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE (IA)

Having established "who" I was deisgning for and "what" the problem is, it was time ​to​ understand “how” users will engage with our content. I began by visualizing​ ​how​ ​I expect​ed ​users ​to​ ​move​ ​through​ ​the​ ​“information​ ​space”​ ​of​ ​navigate our vocabulary app. ​

I achieved this by implementing two common IA techniques, namley task anlayses and user flows. Here I outline two main tasks that Maria will need to accomplish in order to achieve her goals and display its corresponding user flow.

Task analysis 1: create custom study material

Entry point: Open app

Success criteria: New deck saved

  1. Open App;
  2. Onboarding screen:
    1. Sign in if existing user;
    2. Create new account if new user;
    1. Search online for user-rated, existing decks:
      1. Select deck of choice;
      2. Select Save to My Decks;
    2. Choose to create new deck;
      1. Fill in the information for deck title and description (optional);
      2. Optional: Choose to place deck in existing folder(s). If no folders have been created, choose to create new folder;
      3. Add required cards to the deck. For each card perform the following:
        • Add term;
        • Add description;
        • Choose to add photo;
        • Choose to add audio;
        • Choose to import data (text) from document;
        • Customize styles.
  4. Save to My Decks.

Task analysis 2: study a deck

Entry point: Open app

Success criteria: Study session completed

  1. Open App;
  2. Onboarding screen:
    1. Sign in if existing user;
    2. Create new account if new user;
  3. Select deck you want to study;
  4. Select training option;
  5. Customize your training programme with options from:
    1. Question type;
    2. Goal setting;
    3. Answer type;
    4. Card type (i.e. All, Cards not yet learned, Learned cards).
  6. Start training;
  7. Work through questions, marking those learnt along the way;
  8. Review results;
  9. Choose to repeat round or end training.

5| TESTING

USABILITY TESTING

While the in-depth exploratory research methods helped me to better understand the target audience in order to “make the right thing”, I now needed to ensure I was "making the thing right" through usability testing.

I decided it was best to involve users early on and receive real user feedback on the prototype before putting in too much time and resources into developing high-fidelity, polished designs.

I conducted 3 moderated in-person tests and one remote test. The objective of the tests was to evaluate the participants' ability to complete the 6 specific tasks that I had designed for, which were laid out as scenario tasks.

  • Goal: evaluate the learnability and efficiency of the prototype's main features;
  • 3 moderated in-person and 1 remote usability test, circa 30 minutes each;
  • Users were given 6 scenario tasks to perform;
  • Errors were measured using Jakob Nielsen’s scale.

KEY FINDINGS

The results of the usability tests revealed several pain point and areas of confusion by the users which were grouped into 4 main categories:

  • Learnability issues: Users generally found that the app lacked enough instruction on how to complete tasks that were unfamiliar to them.
  • Navigation/ IA issues: An inconsistent bottom tab bar resulted in confusion.
  • Discoverability issues: In some instance, users had trouble locating the right buttons to perform a task.
  • Icon usability issues: The meaning of some non-universal icons were ambiguous to users, causing confusion.

NEXT STEPS & KEY LESSONS

FUTURE STEPS

“When harnessed correctly, technology can enhance lives through healthful behavior that improve our relationships, make us smarter, and increase productivity." (Nir Eyal in Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products)

In the book Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products, Nir Eyal explains that a habit-forming product needs four steps to hook its users: Trigger, Action, Investment, and Variable reward.

In thinking about next steps, I evaluated how the Hook model, displayed below, has been applied in the design of Deck Drills.

Trigger

In terms of triggers, Deck Drills has built in external triggers in the form of regular reminders to users to use the app and attain their learning goal. Internal triggers, on the other hand, com from within the user, which arise from the gratification of doing the task itself. These are designed by making sure Deck Drills connects to the internal goal of the user and what they really want, which is to learn new vocabulary.

Action

This is where usability comes in. To increase thee odds of users using the app, it must be as easy as possible, while simultaneously boosting the user’s motivation.

Investment

Deck Drills requires users to invest time and effort when creating their own decks which helps them to develop strong associations and connections with the vocabulary, as well as the app.

Variable Reward

A future step in developing the design of Deck Drills is to focus on the variable rewards phase of the model. In order to keep users engaged and eager to continue using the app, a points and rewards system needs to be designed. Additionally, social features are key to the variable reward phaser considering that social interaction creates infinite variability and emotional satisfaction. Thus th social aspect of the app needs to be developed. This should include the ability to share achievements with friends, engage in friendly competition, as well as form study buddy groups for support and accountability.

Other steps to consider:

  • Develop wireframes for other key app functionalities, such as creating and uploading notes, viewing flashcards, taking tests, playing games, and the social feature of the app.
  • Perform more usability tests and continue reviewing and refining the prototype.
